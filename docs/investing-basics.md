# TRaid — Investing Basics (plain-English help)

> A growing beginner reference. Plain explanations, honest about limits.
> **Decision-support, not licensed financial advice.**

## The big split

- **PEG** answers *"is this worth owning / is it cheap?"* → **value** (the core of the decision).
- **RSI, Stochastic, MACD** answer *"is now a decent moment?"* → **timing/momentum** (secondary, noisier, contested — treat them as *hints*, never buy buttons).

---

## PEG — "cheap for how fast it grows?"

Start with **P/E** = price ÷ yearly profit per share. It's *how many years of current profit you're paying for one share.* P/E 20 = paying 20 years' worth. But a high P/E isn't automatically expensive — a fast grower *deserves* a higher one.

**PEG fixes that: P/E ÷ growth rate.** It asks *"is the price justified by the growth?"*

- **~1 = fairly priced. Below 1 = cheap for its growth (a real bargain). Above ~1.5–2 = getting pricey.**
- Example: **AVGO at PEG 0.66** = you're paying *less* than its growth rate warrants. That's genuine value.
- **Catch:** it uses *estimated* future growth, which can be wrong. Best single "is this growth stock actually cheap" gauge, but not gospel.

**This is the one that should drive the decision.**

---

Now the three momentum gauges (all "how stretched is the rubber band?"):

## RSI — how hard it's run (0–100)

Measures how strongly price rose vs fell over ~14 days.

- **Over 70 = overbought** (run up fast, may need a breather).
- **Under 30 = oversold** (beaten down, may bounce). 30–70 = neutral.
- **We look for:** a *low* RSI can flag a beaten-up name to buy; a *high* one says don't chase.
- **Catch:** overbought can *stay* overbought for weeks in a strong trend. A hint, not a timer.

## Stochastic — where price sits in its recent range (0–100)

Cousin of RSI, but it asks: is today's close near the **top or bottom** of its recent high-low range?

- Near the top → high (**over 80 = overbought**). Near the bottom → low (**under 20 = oversold**).
- **We look for:** same idea — low = possibly washed out, high = stretched.
- **Catch:** twitchy and noisy; pins at extremes in strong trends. Lowest weight of the bunch.

## MACD — is momentum turning up or down?

Compares a short-term price average to a longer-term one. Two lines (MACD + a "signal" line) and bars showing the gap.

- **MACD crosses *above* its signal = momentum turning up (bullish). Crosses *below* = turning down (bearish).** The bars growing/shrinking show momentum building or fading.
- So "MACD bearish" means momentum is still pointing down — not yet turned up.
- **Catch:** it's built from past averages, so it **lags** — it confirms a turn *after* it starts. Good for "is momentum with me or against me," not precise timing.

---

## How to use them together (for a value buyer)

**PEG decides *what's worth buying*.** Then RSI / Stochastic / MACD just keep you from buying at a euphoric top:

- PEG says cheap **and** RSI/Stoch are low (oversold) → a value buyer's sweet spot.
- PEG says cheap **but** RSI is 75 (hot) → maybe wait, or DCA in gradually.
- **Never** let a green RSI line talk you out of a genuinely cheap, great business for the long run.

## Bonus: "bargain" means cheap vs *worth*, not cheap vs *last week*

At the supermarket, the bargain is "cheaper than the normal price," and milk is identical every time and never appreciates. A stock is different:

- It has **no fixed shelf price** — the quote moves every second, so "up 2% from Tuesday" tells you nothing about value. What matters is cheap **vs what the business is worth** (that's what PEG measures).
- A good business **compounds** — so "wait until it's cheaper than today" often means *it's never that cheap again*, and you miss years of growth. The market rewards owning good businesses early; the fridge doesn't.

So buying a company at a fair-to-cheap valuation is *being the value buyer* — even on a green day.
